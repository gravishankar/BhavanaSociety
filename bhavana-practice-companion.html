Practice Guidance
Understanding what arises in meditation — and how to meet it.
Working with distractions
Thoughts & mind-wandering
The mind wanders — this is its nature. Noticing the wandering is itself awareness. You have not failed; you have awakened.
"Gently, without scolding the mind, return your attention to the breath. Returning is the practice."
Physical discomfort
Bring a curious, gentle attention to the sensation. Notice where it is, its quality, whether it changes. If it is simply posture, adjust slowly and mindfully.
"The body is impermanent. Observe the sensation with equanimity, as you would a cloud passing."
Drowsiness & dullness
Open the eyes slightly. Lift the gaze. Take a few slightly deeper breaths. You can also try standing for a few moments or doing a short walking practice.
"Apply energy — the quality of effort — to the practice. Not forcing, but a gentle brightening of attention."
Restlessness & agitation
Rather than fighting the restlessness, let it be the object. Observe it in the body — where does it live? Is it hot or cool? Tense or scattered? Naming it can loosen its grip.
"Restlessness, I see you. Return to the breath. Return to the body. Return to this moment."
"There will be times when the meditation is difficult — when the mind will not settle and nothing seems to work. This is normal, not a failure. Continue, humbly and patiently, and the clarity will come."
Bhante Gunaratana · Bhavana Society
The five hindrances & their antidotes
Sensual desire
Contemplating impermanence is the classical antidote. Notice the craving as a passing mental event.
Ill-will
Turn to mettā — loving-friendliness. Wish the person or situation well, and release the grip.
Sloth-torpor
Energize the mind — open the eyes, straighten the posture, breathe deeply, or walk.
Restlessness-worry
Settle by anchoring firmly in the breath. Trust the process. The mind will eventually calm.
Doubt
Return to what you know from direct experience. Doubt itself is just another mental event — notice it, and continue.
